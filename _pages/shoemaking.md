# From Workshop to Workbench: A Shoemaker's Path into Science

There is a particular satisfaction in holding something in your hands that did not exist before. A well-turned joint. A cleanly skived edge. A heel, built up layer by layer, that stands perfectly plumb. It is the same satisfaction as closing a proof. Every step follows inevitably from the last. The result is both surprising and, in hindsight, completely obvious.

This is the story of how I became a Master Shoemaker. And why that matters for the scientist I am today.

## I. The Initial Conditions

Every derivation begins somewhere. Mine began in my father's workshop.

Long summers in the countryside left little room for distraction. The workshop was where my creativity could unfold. Wood, tools, the smell of sawdust. By the time I graduated school, I had accumulated serious experience with timber: large structures, furniture, anything that required patience and a steady hand.

Then, one afternoon, I found a book about bespoke shoemaking on my father's bookshelf.

I opened it and was immediately confronted with a humbling realisation. I knew almost nothing about leather. I had absolutely no idea how a shoe was made. That gap between curiosity and knowledge was exactly the kind of problem I could not leave unsolved.

## II. The Apprenticeship and Germany's Dual System

At this point I should mention something that makes Germany genuinely special: the "Duales Ausbildungssystem", the dual vocational training system. Rather than choosing between academic study and practical trade, Germany's system allows you to do both simultaneously. You work in a workshop or company while attending a vocational school. The result is a generation of craftspeople who are not merely skilled with their hands, but who understand the theory and business behind their craft. It is, in my view, one of Germany's most underappreciated institutions.

It was through this system that I began my apprenticeship in 2012 at a bespoke shoemaking workshop in Neumünster, a 45-minute drive from home. Bespoke shoemakers are already a rare species, so it struck me as curious that Neumünster had two. The city has a deeply industrial history. It was a centre of textile and leather production through much of the 20th century. The tanners, the suppliers, the specialist tool shops were all nearby. Proximity to materials is an advantage one does not fully appreciate until one has to source a particular hide on short notice.

## III. Hans-Joachim Vauk

My teacher was Hans-Joachim Vauk.

Hans-Joachim had trained at Bally in Switzerland in the 1970s. Bally was known for producing some of the finest hand-crafted bespoke shoes of that era. He had run his own workshop for over 40 years when I arrived as an intern. Watching him work was like watching a great lecturer give a talk, or reading a paragraph written by someone who truly commands their subject. Every cut was deliberate. Every tool was used with a calm, unhurried confidence that comes only from decades of repetition.

Skill at that level is not talent. It is accumulated practice, made invisible by time. Hans-Joachim showed me what mastery actually looks like. That lesson has stayed with me in everything I do since.

I finished my apprenticeship in 2014, graduating as best of my class. My "Gesellenstück", the journeyman's piece, was a pair of shoes completed within a single week. It had to demonstrate not only quality, but also speed and economical working. It was the first formal proof that I could produce under pressure.

## IV. The Master's Examination

During the first year of my PhD, I recognised a closing window of opportunity.

Germany's Master craftsman examination in bespoke shoemaking requires five certified Master Shoemakers as examiners, for both the theoretical and practical components. As the craft contracts and workshops close, assembling such a panel becomes increasingly difficult. I knew this might be my last realistic chance.

For six months, I spent every weekend travelling to Berlin to attend the Master's class. The examination covers the theoretical and technical mastery of the craft.

My "Meisterstück", the master's piece, is shown below. It represents everything the preceding years had taught me about patience, precision, and the willingness to redo something until it is right.

At that point I had passed the technical examination and completed my Meisterstück. The two remaining modules, on pedagogy and business management, I completed after finishing my PhD in 2025. Both have quietly influenced how I supervise people and how I set priorities.


## V. Forschung Aktuell

There is one detail of the workshop years I have not yet mentioned.

Every day, while cutting, clicking, skiving, stitching, lasting, welting, attaching soles, building heels, trimming edges and polishing leather, I listened to "Deutschlandfunk, Forschung aktuell" (Science Today), the German public radio science programme. It was the highlight of the working day. Climate, physics, biology, mathematics. The world of ideas arrived in the workshop every afternoon.

I had known before starting the apprenticeship that I wanted to study at a university one day, as my friends were doing. Forschung aktuell helped me decide that it would be physics.

## VI. Two Hearts

Until today, two hearts beat in my chest: one for science, one for the craft.

They are not in competition. They are in equilibrium. That equilibrium is what keeps both alive. After long weeks at the desk, I need the workbench. The smell of leather, the resistance of the lasting pliers, the silence of focused handwork. It clears the mental disk space. After time in the workshop, I return to the desk with renewed motivation.

Some people cycle through the countryside. Others read, or walk. I go into the workshop, feel the leather, and reach for my tools.

## VII. The Conclusion

Every derivation must arrive somewhere.

Having completed both my PhD and my Master's degree in shoemaking, I have made a decision. My future lies in atmospheric physics, with a focus on climate change and extreme events. The craft taught me precision, patience, and the humility of standing before something I do not yet understand. Science gives me the tools to ask questions whose answers might genuinely matter for more people than I could ever reach at the workbench.

The workshop will always be there. But the atmosphere is changing. And that is where I want to be.
